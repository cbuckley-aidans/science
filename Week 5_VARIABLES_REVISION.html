Science - Scientific Investigations
Identifying Variables
Independent, dependent, and controlled variables across three experiments
Learning Intention
What are we learning today?
Learning Intention
I am learning to identify the independent, dependent, and controlled variables in a scientific experiment.
Success Criteria
I can define independent, dependent, and controlled variables.
I can correctly identify each type of variable when given an experiment description.
I can explain why the independent variable is plotted on the x-axis and the dependent variable on the y-axis.
Three types of variable
Key idea
In a fair test, we carefully control which things change and which things stay the same. Each variable in an experiment has a specific role.
Independent
The variable you deliberately change. It is the thing being tested. There is only ever one independent variable in a fair test.
Dependent
The variable you measure or observe. It is the result. It depends on what you changed - hence the name.
Controlled
Everything you keep the same each trial. These make the test fair and ensure your results are actually caused by the independent variable.
Experiment 1 - Toy Car & Surfaces
Let me walk through this one.
I Do
Scenario
A student wants to know if the type of surface affects how far a toy car travels after rolling down a ramp. She rolls the same car down the same ramp at the same angle, but places different surfaces at the bottom: carpet, tiles, and sandpaper. She measures the distance the car travels after leaving the ramp.
Think: what is she deliberately changing? what is she measuring? what must stay the same to make it a fair test?
IV
Type of surface at the bottom of the ramp - she deliberately changes this each trial: carpet, tiles, sandpaper.
DV
Distance the car travels after leaving the ramp - this is what she measures and records as the result.
CV
Controlled: same car, same ramp, same angle, same ramp height, same starting position, same length of surface being tested.
Strategy: ask yourself "what else could affect the result?" - everything on that list is a controlled variable.
Putting it on a graph
The Rule
X-axis (horizontal)
Independent variable - what you controlled and changed.
Y-axis (vertical)
Dependent variable - what you measured as a result.
The surface type is a category, not a number, so we use a bar chart. Each bar shows what happened on that surface.
Memory hook
Dependent goes on the Down axis (vertical). It depends on what you put on the x-axis.
Experiment 2 - Berocca & Water Temperature
Your turn to have a go.
We Do
Scenario
A student is investigating whether the temperature of water affects how quickly an effervescent tablet dissolves. She drops one tablet into cups of water at 10°C, 25°C, and 50°C and times how long it takes for each tablet to fully dissolve. She uses the same brand of tablet and the same volume of water each time.
On your whiteboard: write down the IV, DV, and at least two controlled variables. You have 90 seconds.
IV
Temperature of the water - she deliberately tests three values: 10°C, 25°C, and 50°C.
DV
Time taken for the tablet to fully dissolve - timed and recorded as the result for each trial.
CV
Controlled: brand and size of tablet, volume of water, container used, whether the water is stirred.
Discussion: is stirring a controlled variable here? What would happen to your results if one cup was stirred and another was not?
Before the graph - which axis?
Variable A
Temperature of the water
(10°C, 25°C, 50°C)
Variable B
Time taken to dissolve
(measured in seconds)
On your whiteboard: draw two labelled axes and decide which variable belongs on each one. Be ready to explain your reasoning.
Axes
X-axis: Water temperature (°C) - the independent variable. We chose these values.
Y-axis: Time to dissolve (s) - the dependent variable. It changed in response.
Temperature is a number, so we use a line graph.
Experiment 3 - Plants & Light Hours
Now you apply it.
You Do
Scenario
A group of students wants to find out if the amount of light a plant receives affects how much it grows in two weeks. They set up five identical pots with the same type of seedling and the same amount of soil and water. Each pot is placed under a lamp for a different number of hours each day: 0, 4, 8, 12, and 16 hours. After two weeks they measure the height of each plant.
On your whiteboard: write the IV, DV, and as many controlled variables as you can find. You have two minutes.
IV
Number of hours of light per day - five conditions: 0, 4, 8, 12, and 16 hours.
DV
Height of the plant after two weeks - measured in cm and recorded as the result.
CV
Controlled: type of seedling, amount of soil, amount of water, type of lamp, pot size, room temperature.
Notice: five conditions instead of three. More conditions gives a clearer picture of the relationship - but there is still only one independent variable being changed.
What does this look like as a graph?
X-axis
Hours of light per day - the independent variable. We chose these values, so they go on the horizontal axis.
Y-axis
Plant height after two weeks (cm) - the dependent variable. It changed in response to the light, so it goes on the vertical axis.
Because the independent variable is a number (continuous), not a category, we use a line graph rather than a bar chart.
